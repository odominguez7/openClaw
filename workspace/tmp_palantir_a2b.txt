Palantir Technologies was founded in May 2003 by Peter Thiel, Alex Karp, Stephen Cohen, and
Joe Lonsdale, emerging from a vision to apply data-analysis tools—similar to PayPal’s fraud
detection—to national security and counter-terrorism efforts (fiisual, Wikipedia). Over time, the
company evolved into a major provider of data-integration and analytics platforms. Its flagship
products include Gotham, which serves intelligence and defense clients (notably U.S. military
and intelligence agencies), Foundry, which caters to commercial and civil-government users,
Apollo as its continuous deployment backbone, and its more recent Artificial Intelligence
Platform (AIP), which incorporates large-language models and generative AI into operations
(Wikipedia). Initially dominant in defense and intelligence, Palantir has significantly expanded
into commercial sectors, powering clients like Morgan Stanley, Merck KGaA, Airbus, and
various public-health entities (Wikipedia).
Palantir’s engagement model with customers blends aspects of both consulting and software
industries. Traditionally, Palantir would send in small, highly technical deployment teams—
often composed of engineers and data scientists—to work side-by-side with clients, tailoring
Gotham or Foundry to their data systems and operational needs. This intensive, almost
consulting-like approach helped the company integrate deeply into critical workflows,
particularly in defense, intelligence, and healthcare. However, in recent years Palantir has sought
to reduce its reliance on this hands-on model by developing standardized offerings and
“bootcamps,” where potential clients can rapidly prototype AI-driven use cases with Palantir’s
Artificial Intelligence Platform (AIP) and Foundry. These programs compress sales cycles,
demonstrate quick value, and enable broader adoption without requiring long, labor-intensive
consulting engagements. In practice, Palantir remains more embedded than a typical software
vendor—its tools often become mission-critical infrastructure—but its trajectory suggests a
transition toward a platform-as-a-service model, where recurring software subscriptions drive
growth while deployment support plays a secondary, accelerative role.
Palantir has experienced rapid growth, with revenues rising from approximately $1.90 billion in
2022 to $2.22 billion in 2023, and first-quarter 2025 revenues soaring 39% year-over-year to
about $884 million—including a 71% jump in U.S. commercial revenue and 45% in government
revenue (Growth Shuttle). The company’s stock price surged 80% in the first half of 2025,
fueled by strong momentum in both government contracts and commercial deployment of its AI

Endogenous and Exogenous Sources of Growth for Palantir
A company’s growth, or decline, could be attributed to endogenous or exogenous mechanisms.
Exogenous mechanisms are those consequential factors outside the control of company
management, and may show up as unexpected shocks or longer-term trends impacting the firm.
For example, pandemic, an unexpected shock, might have increased demand for online service
infrastructure, and thus boosted demand for Palantir’s services. On the other hand, the growth in
AI capabilities, a trend, might have been boosting the prospects for Palantir’s AI integration
services. Critically, companies may identify trends, and the underlying feedback mechanisms
generating those, and align their strategies with those trends. However, planning for unexpected
shocks is often more limited to building resilience and responding to exogenous changes when
they materialize.
While important, both trends and shocks are not usually enough for understanding the long-term
patterns of growth. They often portray managers as reactive to external events rather than
designing strategies that shape the path for the company, and thus may miss important leverage

points beyond market selection or building resilience. Endogenous drivers of growth are those
that materialize in feedback loops connecting firm-level actions and resources, often intertwined
with broader market and environment in which the firm is situated. Understanding and designing
growth pathways requires the identification of endogenous, often reinforcing, feedback loops
that may drive growth in a firm’s product quality, capacity, market size, etc. Effective strategy
design and execution also requires the identification of endogenous mechanisms that can limit
the growth of the firm at some point, so that those emerging balancing loops are anticipated, and
responded to before they surprise the firm. In short, identifying endogenous sources of growth
and decline is at the heart of building a systemic view of firm strategy.
In this assignment you are seeking an endogenous explanation for Palantir’s recent growth, and
potential limits to it. That endogenous explanation must consist of different feedback processes,
not exogenous shocks like the pandemic. They could also include feedback processes that have
fueled trends on which Palantir might ride.
